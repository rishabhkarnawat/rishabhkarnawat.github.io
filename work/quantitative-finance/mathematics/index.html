Probability & Statistics

Every strategy in quantitative finance is ultimately a bet on a probability distribution. Before you can price a derivative, build a factor model, or test a mean-reversion signal, you need a precise language for describing uncertainty. That language is probability theory.

Random Variables

A random variable is a number whose value is determined by some process. Tomorrow's stock price is a random variable. So is the number of trades executed in the next hour, or the profit from a position you have not yet closed. By convention, random variables are written with capital letters: X, Y, Z. Their realized values are written in lowercase: x, y, z.

Probability Distributions

A probability distribution is a complete map of uncertainty — the full range of possible outcomes and how likely each one is. For a discrete variable (like the number of heads in ten coin flips), this is the probability mass function (PMF). For a continuous variable (like a stock return), it is the probability density function (PDF). The PDF does not give you the probability of an exact value — it gives you the probability of landing in a range. The probability that X falls between a and b is the area under the curve from a to b.

The Normal Distribution

The normal distribution X ∼ N(μ, σ²) is the workhorse of quantitative finance. It appears everywhere for three reasons: mathematical convenience, the Central Limit Theorem (the sum of many small independent random variables converges to normal), and the fact that it is fully described by just two parameters — mean μ and variance σ². Log-returns on assets are approximately normally distributed over short intervals, which is why nearly every model in this field starts there.

Expectation

The expected value of a random variable is the probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes. For a discrete variable:

E[X] = ∑i xi × P(X = xi)

For a continuous variable with PDF f(x):

E[X] = ∫−∞+∞ x · f(x) dx

Linearity of expectation is one of the most useful properties in all of finance: E[aX + bY] = a E[X] + b E[Y], regardless of whether X and Y are correlated.

Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance measures how spread out a distribution is around its mean. It is defined as the expected squared deviation:

Var[X] = E[(X − E[X])²] = E[X²] − (E[X])²

Standard deviation is the square root of variance, which restores the original units. In finance, the standard deviation of returns is called volatility. A stock with 20% annual volatility has a standard deviation of log-returns equal to 0.20 per year.

Std[X] = σ = √Var[X]

Covariance

Covariance measures how two variables move together. Positive covariance means they tend to move in the same direction; negative covariance means they tend to move in opposite directions.

Cov[X, Y] = E[(X − E[X])(Y − E[Y])]

Correlation

Correlation normalizes covariance to the range [−1, +1], making it scale-invariant and directly comparable across different pairs of assets:

Corr[X, Y] = Cov[X, Y] / (Std[X] × Std[Y])  ∈  [−1, +1]

Correlation = +1 means the two variables always move together perfectly. Correlation = −1 means they always move in exactly opposite directions. Correlation = 0 means no linear relationship. This matters enormously for portfolio construction: holding two assets with correlation −1 provides perfect hedging.

Exercise 1 — Portfolio Volatility

You hold 50% in Stock A (σ = 10%) and 50% in Stock B (σ = 10%). The portfolio variance formula is:

Portfolio σ = √(wA²σA² + wB²σB² + 2 wA wB Cov[A,B])

With correlation = +1, the portfolio σ = 10% (no diversification). With correlation = 0, portfolio σ = 7.07% (the √2 reduction). With correlation = −1, portfolio σ = 0% (perfect hedge). Try this in the Correlation Explorer in Part 4.

Stochastic Processes

A stochastic process is a sequence of random variables indexed by time. Asset prices are stochastic processes — they are not deterministic functions of time, but evolve randomly according to some underlying distribution. The key challenge is modeling that evolution in a way that is both mathematically tractable and empirically reasonable.

Random Walks

The simplest model for a price process is the symmetric random walk:

X(t+1) = X(t) + ε(t)     where ε(t) = ±1 with equal probability

The key insight is that uncertainty compounds over time. After T steps, the expected absolute displacement is proportional to √T, not T. If you walk randomly for 4 days, your expected distance from the start is twice what it would be after 1 day — not four times. This √T scaling is the fundamental characteristic of random processes and explains why long-horizon volatility grows slower than linearly.

Brownian Motion

Brownian motion W(t) is the continuous-time limit of the random walk. It satisfies three properties:

1. W(0) = 0    2. Increments are independent    3. W(t) ∼ N(0, t)

The variance grows linearly with time, so the standard deviation grows as √t. An increment over a small interval dt is written dW, and it satisfies:

dW ∼ N(0, dt)   →   Var[dW] = dt

The fact that (dW)² = dt in expectation is the key insight that drives Ito's Lemma. This term does not vanish as dt → 0, which makes stochastic calculus fundamentally different from ordinary calculus.

Ito's Lemma

If f is a smooth function of a Brownian motion W and time t, then its differential is:

df = ∂f/∂t · dt  +  ∂f/∂W · dW  +  (1/2) · ∂²f/∂W² · dt

The extra term (1/2)(∂²f/∂W²)dt is the Ito correction. It has no analogue in ordinary calculus and arises precisely because (dW)² = dt. Without it, every financial model built on Brownian motion would be systematically wrong.

Geometric Brownian Motion

The standard model for stock prices is Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM). Rather than modeling the price S(t) directly, we model the percentage change:

dS = μ · S · dt  +  σ · S · dW

Here μ is the drift (expected return per unit time) and σ is the volatility. The S multiplier ensures prices are always positive. Applying Ito's Lemma to f(S) = log(S):

d(log S) = (μ − σ²/2) dt  +  σ dW

This is the crucial result. Log-returns are normally distributed with mean (μ − σ²/2) per unit time and variance σ² per unit time. The σ²/2 term is called the Ito correction or volatility drag: even if the expected arithmetic return is μ, the realized compound return over time is μ − σ²/2. This is not a modeling artifact — it is a mathematical fact about compounding in the presence of volatility.

In Python, a discrete simulation of GBM uses the exact solution to the SDE:

# Simulate N paths of GBM over T years import numpy as np def simulate_gbm(S0, mu, sigma, T, n_steps=252, n_paths=10): dt = T / n_steps Z = np.random.standard_normal((n_steps, n_paths)) # random shocks log_returns = (mu - 0.5 * sigma**2) * dt + sigma * np.sqrt(dt) * Z log_price = np.cumsum(log_returns, axis=0) return S0 * np.exp(log_price) # shape: (n_steps, n_paths)

Time Series

A time series is a sequence of observations indexed by time. The statistical tools built for time series differ from cross-sectional statistics in one fundamental way: observations are not independent. Yesterday's return predicts something about today's. Understanding these dependencies — and detecting when they break down — is the core of systematic trading.

Stationarity

A time series X(t) is weakly stationary if three properties hold for all t and all lags k:

E[X(t)] = μ      (constant mean) Var[X(t)] = σ²     (constant variance) Cov[X(t), X(t+k)] = γ(k)    (covariance depends only on lag, not time)

Stock prices are not stationary. A price of 150 today does not revert toward some fixed mean; it can drift to any level. Log-returns, however, are approximately stationary — a return of 2% today is not systematically different from a 2% return ten years from now. Regressing two non-stationary series on each other produces spurious regression: you will find a statistically significant relationship even when none exists, simply because both series share a common drift. Test for stationarity with the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test before any regression involving price levels.

# Augmented Dickey-Fuller test for stationarity from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller result = adfuller(price_series, autolag='AIC') adf_stat, p_value = result[0], result[1] # H0: series has a unit root (non-stationary) # Reject H0 if p_value < 0.05 print(f"ADF statistic: {adf_stat:.3f}, p-value: {p_value:.4f}") if p_value < 0.05: print("Series is stationary (reject unit root)") else: print("Series is non-stationary (fail to reject unit root)")

Autocorrelation

The autocorrelation function (ACF) measures the correlation of a series with its own past values:

ACF(k) = Corr[X(t), X(t−k)] = γ(k) / γ(0)

ACF at lag k tells you how correlated today's value is with the value k periods ago. For a white-noise series (no predictability), all ACF values should be near zero. Positive ACF at short lags suggests momentum — recent moves tend to continue. Negative ACF suggests mean reversion — recent moves tend to reverse. Both are tradeable patterns, but they require different strategies: trend-following for positive ACF, mean-reversion for negative.

Cointegration

Two non-stationary series X(t) and Y(t) are cointegrated if there exists a linear combination that is stationary:

Z(t) = Y(t) − β · X(t)    is stationary

The coefficient β is called the cointegrating coefficient. It represents the long-run equilibrium relationship between the two series. If Z(t) drifts far from its mean, it will eventually revert — the two series are held together by an underlying economic force. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, for example, are both large US investment banks with similar macro exposures. Their prices may each be non-stationary individually, but the spread between them is held stationary by the fact that they face the same systematic forces.

Pairs Trading Workflow

Cointegration is the foundation of pairs trading:

# Step 1: Test for cointegration between two price series from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import coint import numpy as np score, p_value, _ = coint(price_A, price_B) # p_value < 0.05 suggests cointegration # Step 2: Estimate the cointegrating coefficient via OLS from numpy.linalg import lstsq beta = lstsq(price_A.reshape(-1,1), price_B, rcond=None)[0][0] # Step 3: Compute the spread and z-score spread = price_B - beta * price_A z_score = (spread - spread.mean()) / spread.std() # Step 4: Signal generation # Entry: |z_score| > 2.0 (spread is 2 standard deviations from mean) # Exit: |z_score| < 0.5 (spread has reverted) long_B = z_score < -2.0 # B cheap relative to A short_B = z_score > 2.0 # B expensive relative to A

The critical failure mode for cointegration-based strategies is structural breaks. During the 2020 pandemic, many pairs that had been cointegrated for decades broke apart permanently as the pandemic hit different businesses differently. When the cointegrating relationship breaks, the spread does not revert — it simply walks away. Any strategy built on cointegration must include stop-loss logic and ongoing monitoring of whether the relationship remains intact.

Linear Algebra

When you move from two assets to hundreds, the two-variable correlation formula becomes unwieldy. Linear algebra provides the compact notation and the computational tools to handle arbitrary numbers of assets simultaneously. Two tools matter most: the covariance matrix and principal component analysis.

The Covariance Matrix

For N assets, all pairwise variances and covariances are organized into a symmetric N×N matrix Σ:

Σ = [Var(1)    Cov(1,2)   …   Cov(1,N)] [Cov(2,1)   Var(2)    …   Cov(2,N)] [⋮] [Cov(N,1)   Cov(N,2)   …   Var(N) ]

The diagonal entries are the variances of each asset. The off-diagonal entries are the covariances between pairs. The matrix is symmetric because Cov(i,j) = Cov(j,i). In Python:

# Estimate covariance matrix from return data import numpy as np # returns: shape (T, N) — T observations, N assets cov_matrix = np.cov(returns.T) # shape: (N, N) # Two-asset example: Stock A (20%), Stock B (30%), corr = 0.5 sigma_A, sigma_B, rho = 0.20, 0.30, 0.5 cov_AB = rho * sigma_A * sigma_B cov_matrix = np.array([[sigma_A**2, cov_AB ], [cov_AB, sigma_B**2]])

Portfolio Variance

For a portfolio with weight vector w (the fraction allocated to each asset), the portfolio variance is the quadratic form:

Portfolio Variance = wT Σ w

For a two-asset portfolio with w = [0.5, 0.5], σA = 20%, σB = 30%, and Cov = 0.02:

w = np.array([0.5, 0.5]) cov_matrix = np.array([[0.04, 0.02], [0.02, 0.09]]) portfolio_variance = w @ cov_matrix @ w # = 0.0425 portfolio_std = np.sqrt(portfolio_variance) # ≈ 20.6%

The portfolio volatility of 20.6% is less than the simple average of 25%. That reduction comes from diversification — the fact that the two assets do not move perfectly together. The correlation slider below shows how this changes as the relationship between the two assets shifts.

Principal Component Analysis

PCA finds the directions in the return space along which variance is maximized. It decomposes the covariance matrix into its eigenvectors and eigenvalues:

Σ · v = λ · v

Each eigenvector v is a principal component — a portfolio of assets with specific loadings. The corresponding eigenvalue λ is the variance explained by that component. In a portfolio of 500 stocks, the first principal component typically accounts for 30–50% of total variance and corresponds to the broad market factor: when it goes up, most stocks go up.

# PCA on a covariance matrix from numpy.linalg import eigh eigenvalues, eigenvectors = eigh(cov_matrix) # Sort by eigenvalue (largest first) idx = eigenvalues.argsort()[::-1] eigenvalues = eigenvalues[idx] eigenvectors = eigenvectors[:, idx] # columns are principal components # Variance explained by each PC explained_variance_ratio = eigenvalues / eigenvalues.sum() print(f"PC1 explains {explained_variance_ratio[0]:.1%} of variance") print(f"PC2 explains {explained_variance_ratio[1]:.1%} of variance")

In practice, PCA is used to reduce a 500×500 covariance matrix to 10–15 risk factors that explain 80%+ of variance. This solves two problems: estimation noise (estimating 125,000 individual covariances reliably requires far more data than is available) and computation (optimizing a portfolio over 10 factors is vastly faster than over 500 assets). The loadings of each stock on the first few components also reveal which macro forces dominate its returns — sector exposure, interest rate sensitivity, growth vs. value tilt.

Synthesis: The Four Tools in One Strategy

These four mathematical frameworks are not independent. A pairs trading strategy uses all of them simultaneously.

Probability and statistics define the signal: compute the spread, estimate its mean and standard deviation, express entry and exit as z-score thresholds. If the spread is more than 2 standard deviations from its mean, the probability of reversion (under the assumed distribution) justifies the trade.

Stochastic processes model the dynamics: the spread between two cointegrated assets follows an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, the mean-reverting analogue of Brownian motion. Ito's Lemma is used to derive the theoretical holding time and expected P&L as a function of the model parameters.

Time series provide the empirical tests: the ADF test confirms the spread is stationary, not just temporarily near its mean; the cointegration test confirms the relationship is structural; the ACF at short lags informs the optimal holding period.

Linear algebra ensures diversification: PCA of the covariance matrix of all current positions checks that different pairs do not all load heavily on the same first principal component (the market factor). If they do, you are running a concentrated market-beta bet disguised as a market-neutral strategy.

Understanding any one of these tools in isolation is not difficult. What makes quantitative finance hard — and valuable — is knowing how they fit together, and which assumptions each one is smuggling in.
